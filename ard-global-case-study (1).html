01 Overview

HR running on
WhatsApp

ARD Global is one of Pakistan's largest real estate firms — a workforce spread across multiple sites, departments, and seniority levels. Their HR function was buried in paper trails, phone calls, and manual spreadsheet tracking. Employees didn't know their leave balance. Managers chased attendance reports. Late arrivals went unchecked or inconsistently handled.

The brief was clear: modernise HR operations without disrupting the way people already communicate. No new app. No login portal. No training curve. The solution had to live where the workforce already lived — WhatsApp — and had to work in Urdu, English, and Punjabi, via both text and voice notes.

What was built is a fully AI-powered HR agent: it answers policy questions, manages leave requests end-to-end, tracks attendance from a physical fingerprint machine, and schedules appointments with HR — all through a single WhatsApp number, without ever requiring a human to be in the loop unless a decision demands it.

02 The Story

Before, during,
and after

Before
HR buried under its own paperwork

At a company the size of ARD Global — hundreds of employees across multiple sites — HR was drowning. Not because the team wasn't capable, but because almost everything ran on manual effort and institutional memory.

An employee wanting to know their leave balance had to call HR, wait to get through, and hope whoever answered had the spreadsheet open. Leave requests went through WhatsApp messages to managers, then got noted somewhere, then sometimes got lost. Attendance was tracked from fingerprint machine exports that someone had to download, read, and manually flag.

Late arrivals were inconsistently handled — sometimes messaged, sometimes not, with no running count, no standard process, and no paper trail. Policy questions either went unanswered or required the HR manager's direct attention for things that were already written in a document nobody could easily access.

Before any automation could be built, the underlying data had to be digitised. Almost the entire operations department ran on paper — attendance registers, leave records, policy documents. There was nothing to connect a system to. That digitisation work came first, and it was the most unglamorous part of the project.

There was also a human layer to navigate. Several people in managerial positions were older and openly skeptical — not of the technology, but of whether it would actually hold up in practice. That skepticism wasn't unreasonable. It meant every early output had to be provably correct before trust started to build.

The HR team was spending most of their time being a lookup service for information that already existed — just not in a form anyone could reach.
The shift
The channel was already there. The intelligence wasn't.

Every employee at ARD Global was already on WhatsApp. It was how they communicated with managers, with colleagues, with family — it was the default interface for everything. Any solution that required installing something new, logging into a portal, or learning a system was going to fail on adoption before it ever got started.

The insight was to treat WhatsApp not as a messaging app but as the interface layer for a complete HR backend. The channel was already trusted and habitual — what it needed was intelligence behind it.

But building for a Pakistani workforce meant the system had to genuinely understand how people actually communicate — switching between Urdu, English, and Punjabi mid-sentence, sending voice notes instead of typing, using informal phrasing for formal requests. A rigid keyword-matching bot would fail immediately. It needed to actually understand intent.

One language problem required a hard fix rather than a model fix: when receiving voice notes in Urdu, the LLM was consistently identifying the language as Hindi and responding in Devanagari script — which is unreadable to a Pakistani workforce. The solution was to hardcode the output to Roman Urdu regardless of how the model classified the input. It's not elegant, but it's reliable.

Getting live attendance data required going directly to the fingerprint machine vendor to request a webhook integration. That conversation took time — it wasn't a standard offering — but without it the system would have been dependent on someone manually exporting CSVs, which defeated the point entirely.

The solution wasn't to change how employees interacted with HR. It was to make HR intelligent enough to meet them exactly where they already were.
After
One number handles what used to take a team

Now an employee who wants to know their leave balance sends a WhatsApp message and gets an accurate answer in seconds — in whatever language they asked in. A leave request goes through the same conversation, gets checked against the live record, reaches the manager automatically, and comes back confirmed or denied without the HR team touching it.

When someone clocks in nine minutes late, a message is waiting on their phone before they've reached their desk — with their running monthly count, consistent across the entire organisation, every single time. No exceptions for people who happened to walk past a manager who didn't notice.

The HR team went from fielding the same five questions fifty times a day to doing actual HR work: handling edge cases, supporting employees through complex situations, and focusing on the parts of their job that actually required a human.

The appointment scheduling has two known limitations. The HR manager must keep his calendar updated for the agent to check availability accurately — if a physical meeting isn't logged, the system can double-book it. And spontaneous meetings that aren't scheduled in advance fall outside what the system can see entirely. Both are documented constraints the client accepted rather than workarounds that could be engineered away cleanly.

From a reactive, phone-based service buried in repetition — to a 24/7 intelligent layer that the workforce adopted without a single training session.
03 What it does

Four systems,
one number

Each capability operates independently but shares the same WhatsApp interface, employee context, and language understanding layer.

01  /  HR Query Agent
Instant policy answers, grounded in the company code
Employees send any HR question — leave entitlements, overtime rules, disciplinary procedures, dress code, benefits — and the agent responds by referencing the actual company HR code document. No hallucinations, no guesswork. If the policy exists, the agent cites it. If it doesn't, it escalates to a human. Available 24/7, responds within seconds.
Text Voice note Urdu English Punjabi
02  /  Leave Management
Smart approvals with a human exactly where they belong
Employees request leave in natural language — "I need Friday off" or "Chutti chahiye aglay hafte". The agent checks their remaining leave balance. If leaves are available, it notifies the relevant manager by email awaiting approval. If no leaves remain, it refuses — politely — and offers an emergency escalation path. Every outcome is logged automatically.
Human-in-the-loop Auto-check Escalation path
03  /  Attendance Intelligence
Fingerprint data, processed and acted on automatically
The system connects directly to ARD's on-site fingerprint attendance machines. If an employee clocks in more than 5 minutes past the cutoff, they are automatically marked late and receive a WhatsApp message with their total late-day count for the month. The same logic applies for early check-outs. No manual intervention, no chasing — just consistent, real-time accountability.
Live sync Fingerprint API Auto-message Running count
04  /  Appointment Scheduling
Book time with HR in plain conversation
Employees can schedule appointments with the HR manager through natural language — "I need to meet HR this week about my contract" — and the agent handles the full booking flow: checks availability, proposes slots, confirms, and sends a reminder. No back-and-forth emails, no waiting on hold. HR's calendar is managed automatically from WhatsApp messages.
Natural language Auto-scheduling Reminders
