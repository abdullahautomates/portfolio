01 Overview

A CEO spending his days
in front of a camera

Axiom Strat Cap sells asset management software to financial firms. Their sales process hinged on a VSL — a video sales letter — where the CEO personally walked each prospect through a tailored pitch. Not a generic explainer. A fully customised, 30-minute presentation built around that specific lead's firm, their challenges, and how the product addressed them.

It worked. The conversion rate on these videos was exceptional. The problem was the time it consumed. Every new lead meant the CEO back in front of the camera — scripting, recording, re-recording, editing — for hours. As the pipeline grew, the VSL process became a bottleneck that was physically impossible to scale.

The brief was precise: keep everything that made the VSLs effective — the CEO's voice, his delivery, the personalisation — and eliminate everything that required him to be in the room. The result needed to feel indistinguishable from a video he'd recorded himself.

02 The Story

Before, during,
and after

Before
The most valuable hour of the CEO's day — spent on camera, alone

Every new lead in the pipeline triggered the same sequence. The CEO would research the firm — their AUM, their current tools, their pain points — script a personalised angle, set up the camera, and record. Thirty minutes of material. Usually two or three takes. Then editing, then export, then sending.

The videos were good. Genuinely good. They felt personal because they were personal — the CEO knew the lead's name, their firm, their specific situation. Prospects responded to that. The conversion rate justified the process.

But as inbound volume grew, the math stopped working. A pipeline of ten active leads meant ten VSLs to record. Some weeks, the CEO was spending more time in front of the camera than he was running the company. Leads at the back of the queue waited days for a video that should have reached them the same day they enquired.

The irony was stark — the very thing that made the sales process work was becoming the thing that made it impossible to scale.
The shift
The voice was the product. The recording didn't have to be.

The VSLs worked because of three things: the CEO's voice and delivery, the personalised content built around the lead, and the structural flow of the pitch. Two of those three things are the same for every lead — the voice and the structure. Only the personalised middle changes.

That realisation reframed the entire problem. Instead of asking "how do we help the CEO record faster," the right question was "which parts of this video actually need to be different for each person — and which parts don't?"

The answer: the core pitch, the product walkthrough, the proof points — these are evergreen. The CEO records them once, perfectly, and they never need to be re-recorded. Only the lead-specific segments — the intro that names their firm, the section that addresses their particular situation — need to change. And those segments are short, scriptable, and generatable.

Once you separate the evergreen from the personalised, the solution becomes obvious: clone the voice, generate the custom parts, stitch them together seamlessly.
After
A lead fills a form. Fifteen minutes later, they're watching their VSL.

Now when a lead fills out the intake form, the system reads their responses, generates the personalised script segments in the CEO's cloned voice via ElevenLabs, and uses FFmpeg to stitch those segments seamlessly into the pre-recorded evergreen sections. A finished, 30-minute VSL — fully personalised, sounding exactly like the CEO recorded it this morning — is in the lead's inbox within fifteen minutes.

The CEO recorded the evergreen segments once. His voice, his delivery, his pacing — all preserved in the clone. Every VSL that goes out sounds like him because it is him, just assembled differently each time.

He now runs a larger pipeline than before, responds to every lead the same day, and hasn't sat in front of a camera for a sales video since the system went live. The videos are still personal. The personalisation just happens automatically.

Same voice. Same quality. Same conversion-driving personalisation. Delivered automatically, to every lead, in fifteen minutes flat.

Six stages. No camera. No editor. No waiting. The CEO's voice does everything — the system just assembles it.

Step 03
Voice cloning — trained on the CEO, indistinguishable from the real thing
ElevenLabs was trained on a curated library of the CEO's existing recordings — enough material to capture his cadence, his pauses, his natural variation in emphasis. The clone doesn't just reproduce his voice; it reproduces how he speaks. Custom segment scripts are fed through the clone and rendered as audio that sits seamlessly alongside the segments he recorded himself. Prospects can't tell the difference — because tonally, there isn't one.
